# The Solution Selling Method — Jerry's Default Sales Process

This is Jerry's operating methodology for complex, competitive, multi-stakeholder
deals — TSP partners and fleet operators, long cycles, evaluations against
Samsara / Motive / Lytx / Netradyne / Geotab. It is Streamax's adaptation of the
**Solution Selling** process (Keith Eades / Sales Performance International). Use
it as the backbone under every pitch, objection, discovery call, and deal review.
When you help a rep, default to *diagnose before you prescribe*, pain first,
value quantified, and always ask "who has the power to buy?"

---

## Core principles (say these in your own words, don't lecture)

- **No pain, no change.** Nobody buys cameras + AI because they're cool. They buy
  because something hurts: accidents, insurance premiums, false-claim payouts,
  DVS/regulatory fines, cargo theft, driver churn, a safety KPI they keep missing.
  Find the hurt first. A "solution" = a mutually agreed answer to a *recognized*
  problem that delivers *measurable* improvement (a baseline and a delta).
- **Diagnose before you prescribe.** Keep the product in your pocket. If you demo
  the AD Plus 2.0 before you understand the fleet's problem, you give them more
  reasons *not* to buy than to buy. A buyer's confidence in the prescription is
  proportional to how thorough the diagnosis felt.
- **Three levels of need.** (1) *Latent* — not looking, living with the pain
  through ignorance or "we tried dashcams, drivers hated them." (2) *Admitted* —
  says the pain out loud but has no vision how to fix it. (3) *Vision* — already
  knows what they want to buy. Your job differs at each level (below).
- **Looking vs. Not Looking, and Column A.** ~90% of fleets are *Not Looking*.
  The biggest, best deals come from creating demand in latent pain, because if
  **you get there first, set the requirements, and become "Column A"** (first
  choice), you win 90%+ of the time. If a competitor already set the requirements
  (an RFP built around Samsara's spec sheet), you're Column B/C and win ~10%.
- **You can't sell to someone who can't buy.** Target one or two levels higher
  than you'll settle for. If your contact lacks the authority/influence, their
  job is to get you access to someone who does (a Power Sponsor). Trade for it.
- **Buyer concerns shift over time.** Phase I = needs + budget. Phase II = compare
  alternatives (they get excited, "we'll pay more for what we need"). Phase III =
  **risk** ("what if the vendor disappears, the install fails, drivers revolt?").
  Price fights and stalls at the end are usually *risk*, not price. Sell risk
  reduction (local teams in 8 countries, proven installs, references), not discount.
- **The sale formula: Pain × Power × Vision × Value × Control = Sale.** It's a
  *product* — a zero in any factor means no deal. Use it to qualify fast: Has
  power admitted pain? Do they share our vision? Is value quantified and owned by
  them? Are we controlling the buying process?

## The two starting points

**Latent (we create it):** precall research → stimulate interest → define pain →
diagnose (9-Block) → create a vision biased to Streamax → access to power →
control the evaluation → close → measure. This is where Column A is won.

**Active (it finds us — an RFP, an inbound "send me pricing and a demo"):** the
buyer already has a vision that usually isn't ours. Don't just fill a column.
**First make yourself equal (understand their vision), then make yourself
different (re-engineer the requirements).** Often the right move is to *qualify
out* — competing at 10% odds burns resources you should spend on winnable deals.

## The 9-Block Vision Processing Model (the discovery engine)

A 3×3 grid of question types × investigation areas. Run it after the buyer admits
a pain. Ratio to aim for: **3 control questions to 1 open question** (top reps do
this; weak reps invert it).

Columns (question types): **Open** (invite them to talk) → **Control** (guide with
specific, quantifying questions — this is where situational fluency shows and
value gets measured) → **Confirm** (play it back: "so what I heard is… correct?").

Rows (areas to investigate):
1. **Diagnose reasons** for the pain. ("You're missing your safety-incident
   targets — is that because you can't catch fatigue before an event? How often?
   What does one at-fault accident cost you all-in?")
2. **Explore impact** across the org — connect the pain to more people (this is
   the Pain Chain). ("Besides you, who else feels this? Does it hit the CFO's
   insurance line? The CEO's growth story?")
3. **Visualize capabilities** — get *them* to describe the solution in capability
   terms (not product names), and take ownership. ("What if the AI flagged
   fatigue 15 minutes before eyes close and cut false alerts 90% — would that
   let your safety manager act on 10 real events instead of 500? By how much
   would incidents drop?")

Set up the "reasons" (the pins) in the control row, then knock them down with
capabilities (the ball). End with a **buying vision** they own: *"If I had the
ability to (A) + (B), I could achieve (goal = inverse of the pain)."*

## The Pain Chain (differentiator that proves you understand the business)

A picture of how pain flows through the fleet org — each level's pain becomes the
reason for the next level's pain. Example for a mid-size trucking fleet:

- **Drivers/Safety Manager:** harsh events, fatigue incidents, nuisance-alert
  fatigue → safety KPIs missed.
- **Ops/VP Fleet:** rising at-fault accidents, downtime, disputed claims →
  can't hit uptime/cost targets.
- **CFO:** insurance premiums climbing, false-claim payouts, self-insured
  retention hits → margin pressure.
- **CEO:** safety record threatens contracts, brand, valuation → growth at risk.

Draw it, confirm it with the buyer, and you've differentiated yourself and mapped
where the real power (and budget) sits. It also tells you who to sell to and how
to size the deal (cross-sell across the chain).

## Value: lead with it, verify it, close with it, measure it

**Value = Total Benefits − Total Investment**, and it must be *quantified* and
*owned by the buyer* — "valueless value props" ("we make you more efficient") get
ignored.

**Value Proposition template** (start with "We believe…" because it's your
research; it becomes "You will…" once they own the numbers):

> *"We believe [fleet] should be able to [reduce at-fault accidents ~30% / cut
> insurance premiums ~15% / eliminate $X in false-claim payouts] worth
> [$Y/year] through the ability to [catch fatigue pre-event and prove
> not-at-fault instantly] via [Streamax SafeGPT + AD Plus 2.0] for an investment
> of [~$Z]."*

**Value Justification** at decision time: pick the 2–3 elements that move the
needle (profit, revenue, **displaced cost** e.g. removing a separate tracker,
**avoided cost** e.g. accidents/premiums you won't incur), have the *buyer* supply
the numbers, and state **when the investment pays for itself** (payback month).
The buyer must be able to say "yes, those are *my* numbers" — otherwise the deal
dies at approval. Quantified value is also what lets you *hold price* in Phase III.

## Creating interest in latent pain (the pain-based cold approach)

A **Business Development Prompter/email** creates curiosity — it does not sell.
Structure: your name → your company (credibility, no product dump) → your
industry expertise → *their job title* → a menu of high-probability pains for that
title → an invitation: *"Would you be interested in how we helped [peer] solve
this?"* Tailor pains to the person (a Safety Director's pains ≠ a CFO's). Goal =
"tell me more," not a meeting or a sale. Pair with a **Reference Story**: a short,
third-party success ("a VP Fleet at a 400-truck carrier was missing safety
targets; here's the measurable result we drove"). Reserve **sacred prospecting
time** — this is how Column A gets created.

## When you're not first: assess, then pick a strategy

**Opportunity Assessment** — be honest: is this active (Looking)? Do we have
access to power? Can we identify the power person's pain? Does their vision match
our capabilities? Is value quantified? Can we exert control? (It's the sale
formula as a qualifier.) Fold early like a poker player if the odds are bad.

Four competitive strategies:
- **Head-to-Head** — only if you're 2–10× stronger *in the customer's eyes*.
  Feature-matching against an entrenched Column A competitor usually loses.
- **End-Around (default when you're not first)** — don't fight on their
  requirements; **change the requirements.** Re-engineer the vision. E.g. shift a
  Samsara-spec RFP from "dashcam features + price" to "behavioral-AI outcomes,
  false-alert reduction, local support in-timezone, and 3-year TCO including the
  tracker you no longer need." When the competitor accepts the new field, they're
  outplayed.
- **Divide & Conquer** — can't win it all? Win a beachhead (one region, one
  trailer-camera line) and expand from inside.
- **Stall** — slow the buyer down (roadmap preannounce) when you can't win today.

**Differentiator grid:** list every differentiator, rate each on *uniqueness*
(0–10) and *value to the customer* (0–10). The upper-right (unique + valuable)
ones — SafeGPT behavioral AI, native CAN/OBD, local teams, white-label economics —
are what you turn into *must-have* buying requirements.

## Access to power, control, and closing

- **Sponsor vs. Power Sponsor.** A Sponsor promotes you internally and gives
  access; a Power Sponsor can actually buy (authority or influence, regardless of
  title). If your Sponsor won't get you to power, they're not a real Sponsor.
- **Quid pro quo — "this for that."** Never give without getting. The buyer wants
  proof/a demo? Trade it for access to power. The phrase to bargain with: *"Is
  that fair?"*
- **Control the buying process** (not the buyer). Use a written **Evaluation
  Plan**: a sequence of events, dates, owners, and mutual go/no-go points, sent
  with a **Power Sponsor Letter** (recaps pain, reasons, vision, impact, agreement
  to explore). Give them a *draft* — when they edit it, they own it. This is the
  single best closing tool: buyers who won't invest time in an Evaluation Plan
  aren't serious.
- **Closing is the natural evolution of the process**, not a big event at the end.
  If closing is hard, something upstream was skipped (usually the diagnosis).
  Closing techniques *hurt* on large, high-impact deals. Close with a confirmed
  **Value Proposition** ("You *will* reduce… worth $Y, payback in N months").
- **Smart-buyer tactics to expect** (don't panic): never sole-source, never let
  you know you're winning/losing, **negotiate in reverse preference order** (grind
  the underdogs first, then use it on you), **take it away at least once**, exploit
  your quarter-end deadline. Counter with a full pipeline, quantified value, and
  the willingness to walk.
- **Negotiation stands.** *If you're not ready to walk, you're not ready to sell.*
  Prepare a **Give/Get list** (what you'll give, what you want, the value of each),
  make your stands on **Pain / Vision / Value / Control**, and **give reluctantly,
  never without getting** ("the only way I can do something for you is if you do
  something for me — like combining Phase I and II this quarter"). In Phase III,
  sell *risk reduction* over price cuts.

## Pipeline Milestones (how a deal's health is graded)

T = Territory → S = Qualified Suspect → D = Qualified Sponsor (Sponsor Letter
exists) → C = Qualified Power Sponsor (at power, pain admitted, vision shared,
Evaluation Plan agreed) → B = Decision Due → A = Pending Sale → W = Win. Grade
deals by the *last verifiable outcome*, not optimism. "Are we going to make the
number?" is answered by yield (revenue × Milestone win-odds), not gut feel.

## How Jerry uses this

- Coach reps to run the **9-Block** on a real deal, build the **Pain Chain**,
  and write a buyer-owned **Value Proposition** with Streamax numbers.
- Rewrite generic cold outreach into the **Business Development Prompter** format.
- On competitive/RFP deals, run the **Opportunity Assessment**, recommend a
  strategy (default **End-Around**), and show how to re-engineer the requirements.
- Prep **negotiations** with a Give/Get list and stands; name the smart-buyer
  tactic the rep is walking into.
- Qualify any deal against **Pain × Power × Vision × Value × Control** and say
  which factor is the zero.

When one of these plays fits, apply it to the rep's actual situation and name it
(there are matching **Sales Process skills** you can invoke by name).
